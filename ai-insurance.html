AI for Insurance

Enabling AI powered search for occupation

1 months
Sr. UX Designer
Web | Mobile

Overview

Outlining the development and implementation of an AI-powered search functionality designed to revolutionize how small business owners select their occupation when purchasing insurance. The goal is to move beyond the limitations of traditional ANZSCO code-based searches by enabling users to input diverse information like website URLs, work descriptions, or business details, which the AI will then analyze to suggest relevant and accurate occupation classifications. This aims to improve user experience, reduce misclassification errors, and ultimately streamline the insurance process.

My Role: I was part of the project as a Sr. UX designer and was responsible for UX/UI Design.

Collaboration: Business Stakeholder, Project Manager, and Dev team.

Tools: Figma, Jira, MS Teams

Discovery

For small business owners seeking insurance, selecting the correct occupation is a crucial first step. Traditionally, this process relies on users navigating lengthy, predefined lists of Australian and New Zealand Standard Classification of Occupations (ANZSCO) codes. This manual approach presents several significant challenges:

  • User Frustration and Time Inefficiency: Business owners, often pressed for time, can find it cumbersome and confusing to sift through hundreds of ANZSCO codes to find the one that accurately reflects their business activities.
  • Risk of Misclassification: The rigid nature of predefined lists means that businesses with nuanced or hybrid roles may struggle
  • Policy and Claim Discrepancies: Misclassification can have serious consequences down the line. If a claim is lodged and the declared occupation doesn't accurately reflect the business's actual operations, it can lead to delays, disputes, or even rejection of the claim.
  • Limited Granularity: ANZSCO codes, while comprehensive, may not capture the specific nuances of a small business's day-to-day activities, potentially leading to a generic classification that doesn't fully align with the risk profile.
  • Lost Conversion Opportunities: A frustrating or confusing initial step in the insurance buying process can lead to potential customers abandoning the process altogether.

User Research

We conducted agent interviews and usability testing to observe users current experience, identify frustrations, and understand their mental models when describing their work.
Key questions includeed were:

  • How do you currently select your business occupation when applying for insurance?
  • What challenges have you faced in finding the right occupation category?
  • What information best describes your business activities?
  • How confident are you in the accuracy of your current occupation selection?
Stakeholder Interviews

Collaborating with underwriters, customer support teams, and business analysts to understand the impact of misclassification on their workflows and identify business requirements for the new search functionality.

Competitive Analysis

Examining existing search interfaces and AI-powered suggestion tools in other domains to identify best practices and potential design patterns. Assessing the current occupation selection process against established usability principles to pinpoint areas for improvement.

Key Challenges:

  • Cognitive Load: Sifting through long lists of ANZSCO codes is mentally taxing and prone to error.
  • Lack of Clarity: Users often struggle to understand the nuances of the predefined categories and how they relate to their specific business.
  • Information Scarcity: The current process provides little to no guidance or support in making the right selection.
  • Mistrust: Users may lack confidence in their chosen occupation if they are unsure it accurately reflects their business.

Ideation

The ideation process for the AI-powered search focuses on creating a user-centric and efficient experience:

  • Information Architecture: Planning the flow and structure of the search interface, ensuring clear entry points for different input methods (URL, free text, business details).
  • Input Flexibility: Designing clear and distinct input fields for website URLs, free-text descriptions, and business details, with appropriate guidance for each.
  • AI Transparency: Clearly communicating that the suggestions are AI-powered and providing a confidence score to manage user expectations and build trust.
  • Review and Refinement: Allowing users to review the AI's suggestions, understand the reasoning (potentially through keywords highlighted in their input), and make an informed decision.
  • Handling Ambiguity: Designing clear pathways for users to provide more information or manually search if the AI's suggestions are not satisfactory.
  • Error Prevention: Implementing measures to prevent common input errors and providing helpful error messages when they occur.
  • Mobile Responsiveness: Ensuring the search interface is fully responsive and provides a seamless experience across different devices.

Key success metrics:

The success of the redesigned occupation search will be evaluated using UX-focused metrics:

  • Task Success Rate: Measuring the percentage of users who successfully find and select an appropriate occupation.
  • Time on Task: Tracking the average time users take to complete the occupation selection process.
  • Abandonment Rate: Tracking the percentage of users who abandon the insurance application process at the occupation selection stage.
  • Error Rate: Monitoring the frequency of user errors during the search and selection process.

Design Phase

Developing a clean, intuitive, and trustworthy visual design that aligns with the insurance provider's brand. This includes:

  • Clear Visual Hierarchy: Ensuring important information, like the AI's suggestions and confidence scores, is easily scannable.
  • Consistent Design Patterns: Utilizing familiar UI elements and interaction patterns to enhance usability.
  • Accessibility: Adhering to accessibility guidelines (WCAG) to ensure the search is usable by everyone.
  • Feedback Mechanisms: Providing clear and timely feedback to the user throughout the search process (e.g., loading states, success messages, error handling).

Conclusion

By applying a user-centered design approach, focusing on UX research, iterative prototyping, and usability testing, the AI-powered occupation search can significantly improve the small business insurance application process. The goal is to create an intuitive and trustworthy experience that empowers users to accurately identify their business occupation, leading to greater user satisfaction, reduced errors, and ultimately a more efficient and reliable insurance process for both the customer and the provider. The success of this project will be measured not only by business metrics but also by the positive impact on the user experience.
